№ 04 · Billing

The invisible half of member finance.

Most of the work in a billing system isn’t billing. It’s the adjacent work that keeps the books honest — reconciliation between what we charged and what the carrier paid, disputes when those don’t match, and the reporting that tells leadership which of it is working. That adjacent work was eating up 40% of the billing team’s manual effort. We got rid of it.

01 / Problem

Three tools, one reality.

The billing team at Collective Health was managing their work across three different systems. Reconciliation — matching charges to payments — lived in one. Disputes — the long-tail cases where the numbers didn’t line up — lived in a second. Reporting — the monthly view leadership needed — was built by copying data from the first two into a shared spreadsheet. Each tool had its own vocabulary for the same line item.

The work wasn’t slow because people were slow. It was slow because each billing specialist was doing, in parallel, the invisible job of translating between three systems.

02 / Insight

A state machine, not a list.

The throughline across all three tools was a single entity — a billing line item — that moves through states: pending, reconciled, disputed, adjusted, resolved, reported. Each tool touched different states. Nobody owned the journey.

The design move, once we saw it, was to stop designing three tools and start designing one — a single workspace that followed a line item through its life, with the right surface exposed at each state.

The fastest way to automate a workflow is to make the object it operates on legible.
03 / Design

One workspace, state-aware.

We built a single billing workspace organized around line items. At each state in the line item’s life, the specialist saw only the tools relevant to that state:

  • Pending: a simple reconciliation view, with carrier payments auto-matched against expected amounts where possible.
  • Disputed: a focused dispute view with a timeline of communications, a template library for common responses, and a clear next-action surface.
  • Resolved / Reported: lightweight audit trail, plus the line item’s inclusion in the monthly report generated automatically from the source data.

What went away: the triple-entry of the same line item in different tools, the monthly reporting scramble, the opaque “how did this number get here” question at every quarterly review.

04 / Automation

The 40% came from the boring half.

The headline number — 40% of manual billing work eliminated — didn’t come from the glamorous parts of the tool. It came from the background jobs: auto-matching reconciled line items, auto-generating the monthly close report from the live data, auto-filing resolved disputes instead of asking a specialist to click through. Each one is a small script. Together they are a working week a billing specialist gets back every month.

The design job was less about inventing the automations and more about deciding which ones were safe to automate and which ones needed a human in the loop. Anything with financial risk got explicit specialist review. Anything deterministic and auditable got automated. The line between the two was the product.

05 / Outcome

Reporting as a byproduct of the work.

Before this project, monthly billing reports were a separate deliverable — a document pulled together at the end of the month, from data that had already been transcribed twice. After this project, the monthly report is a read-only view of the live system. It is, in a small but satisfying way, the same job as the day-to-day work.

That pattern — reporting as a byproduct of correctly-shaped data, not a separate deliverable — is something I’ve carried into every internal tool I’ve designed since. Most of the reporting dashboards I’ve built at LifeStance work this way.
