№ 04 · Strategy & ROI

Design as a revenue lever, not a cost center.

Most design teams get discussed in cost terms — headcount, burn, allocation. Late last year, with a new CTO coming in and a resource-planning cycle opening up, I decided to stop arguing that design was valuable and start showing it. I built a financial model of the design team’s ROI, grounded in the shipped projects we could point to, and walked it into the room. It changed the conversation.

01 / Why

Design without a number on it.

Every design leader I’ve worked with has some version of the same conversation with their CFO: “We know you’re doing good work, but we can’t quite see the impact in the model.” That’s a fair critique, and the response most teams reach for — a deck of before/after screenshots, a few testimonial quotes from product partners — doesn’t really answer it.

I wanted to answer it. Not with vibes. With a model a finance team could redline.

02 / Method

Build it on the ledger, not the mood board.

I started with the shipped work the team could clearly claim an outcome on. For each project, I wrote down three things: the directly-attributable savings or revenue (the hard number), the reasonable inferred upside (the softer but defensible one), and the assumptions behind both — so anyone reading the model could argue with them instead of with me.

The big entries

  • Matching tool time savings — a measured 39-second per-call handle-time reduction across a 33-state rollout, isolated by a natural control group. Modeled downstream two ways, both shown: reclaimed coordinator labor at fully-loaded cost, and the booking capacity that time opens up at average visit revenue. The dollar figures are potential, not booked; the assumptions are on the same page as the number. The largest single input to the model. (See Matching Tool.)
  • Matching framework lift — estimated conversion gains on specific funnel stages attributable to design interventions at each gate.
  • Operational unlocks — capacity returned to care coordinators, clinicians, and call-center agents from tool consolidation, priced at fully-loaded labor.
  • Avoided build — engineering work the team didn’t have to do because a design iteration ruled it out before the commit.

On the cost side: fully-loaded comp, tooling, and a fair share of managerial overhead. No thumb on the scale.

03 / Result

A realized 9–11×, and a scoped 40–100×.

The realized ROI — the number the current team, at the current headcount, against the current cost, could point to in shipped work — came out to 9–11×. That’s a real multiple, and it isn’t a rounding error on the P&L.

The scoped upside was the more interesting number. With two senior hires (a platform designer and a second researcher) and a shift in what the team worked on — away from incremental feature polish, toward the structural internal-tools and routing work that historically had the highest ROI — the model supported a 40–100× multiple. Not tomorrow. Over the investment horizon the CTO was planning against.

A design team that can show its work on a spreadsheet is a design team that gets invested in.
04 / Use

It became the planning tool.

The model landed during the new CTO’s first resource-planning cycle. It didn’t just get looked at once and filed — it became the working document, the thing the CTO and I referenced when deciding which design initiatives to staff, which to defer, and where engineering capacity should flow against them.

More importantly, it became a shared vocabulary between product, design, and engineering leadership. A prioritization framework fell out of it almost accidentally: for any new initiative, we asked three questions — what’s the direct financial lift, what’s the operational unlock, and which design capacity does it require — and ranked the portfolio against those. That framework now runs quarterly planning across all three functions.

05 / Reflection

What I took away from it.

Two things, both of which I’d carry into any future design leadership role.

First: the model doesn’t have to be perfect. It has to be inspectable. The assumptions being visible is what makes a finance team trust it — not the sophistication of the math. The first draft I walked in with had plenty of numbers people argued with, and that was fine. The argument itself moved design closer to the center of resource planning, not further from it.

Second: most of the highest-leverage design work in a healthcare-operations org looks boring from the outside. Routing rules. Reconciliation dashboards. A single screen where an agent enters a claim. The ROI conversation pushed me to be more honest about which projects I wanted to staff next — and the answer was almost always the less glamorous one.
