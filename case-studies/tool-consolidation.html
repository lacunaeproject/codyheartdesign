№ 02 · Tool Consolidation

Fifty tools, six jobs, one platform.

Intake coordinators at LifeStance juggled 50+ regional tools while live on the phone with patients seeking mental health care. Every region had built its own workarounds, and every call paid for it in time. Documenting the full stack nationwide showed something the org couldn’t see from any single region: 50 tools were doing six underlying jobs. That reframe — 50 to 6 — turned tool consolidation from an intractable landscape into an ordered roadmap. “One tool” here isn’t one interface; it’s one system of record for those six jobs. Today it’s a platform. Tomorrow it could be an agent, an API, or something we haven’t named. The consolidation logic is the same.

Solution

Fifty tools, six jobs.

Documenting every tool the intake team used, nationwide, showed a shape the org couldn’t see from any single region: the 50+ regional tools were solving six underlying jobs — finding a good-fit provider, priority providers and hotlists, insurance and credentialing, provider preferences, referrals, and intake capture. That reframe turned consolidation from an intractable landscape into an ordered roadmap: fold those six jobs into one system of record, in a priority order driven by where coordinators actually lose time.

01 / Origin

Where the 50 tools came from.

LifeStance grew clinic-first, and every region solved its own intake problems locally. BI matching dashboards, spreadsheet credentialing trackers, priority-provider hotlists, regional quick guides. Each existed because it solved a real problem for its region. The cost showed up on the phones, where coordinators had to juggle them all.

Who pays for that time: intake coordinators. An IC is live on the phone, cycling between tools to answer four questions at once. Who’s a clinical fit. Who takes this insurance. Who has availability. Who the operation needs booked.

My role: design lead on an internal platform that matches patients with clinicians during these calls. This work ran alongside the platform’s pilot, with a clear goal — understand the full tool stack ICs actually used, then turn that into a consolidation strategy the company could act on.

02 / Inventory

The first complete map of intake tooling.

I documented every tool in the company, nationwide. Working with my intake SME, I got access to each one and categorized it by state, region, scope, purpose, and priority. Slow, manual work. It gave the company its first complete map of its own intake tooling.

The map showed the tools had a shape. Nearly all of them did one of six jobs:

  • Finding a good-fit provider
  • Priority providers and hotlists
  • Insurance and credentialing checks
  • Provider preferences
  • Referrals
  • Chart creation and intake capture

Fifty tools doing six jobs. That made consolidation look tractable.

03 / Vision

One tool — and what that actually means.

One tool. A stakeholder workshop surfaced five priority areas; four depended on other teams, so I took the one design could drive directly. The play was narrower than a platform rebuild: every regional tool patched the same gaps in eligibility, cost, fit, availability, and preferences. Solve those in one place, inside the matching platform, and the 50-tool landscape stops needing to exist.

“One tool” here isn’t one interface. It’s one system of record for the six jobs. Today that’s a matching platform coordinators open during a call. Tomorrow it could be an agent surfacing the same information conversationally, or an API another workflow calls into. The consolidation logic — six jobs, one source of truth — is the same regardless of embodiment. The interface is the current implementation of the vision, not the vision itself.

04 / Priority

What to consolidate first.

Priority order from the inventory, interviews, round tables, and shadowing. It follows where ICs actually lose time.

1 · Insurance — highest impact

The failure loop: find a match, check insurance in a separate tracker, discover the provider doesn’t accept the plan, start over. Medicare and Medicaid calls run 15+ minutes inside spreadsheets. EAP booking needs five data points, and most callers are missing at least one.

Don’t get me wrong, booking would definitely help — but we spend so much time in these spreadsheets, it wouldn’t help in the areas we’re having problems.

That answer reordered my roadmap. Recommendation: consolidate 10+ insurance tools into one; screen coverage early in the call so ICs can give answers fast.

2 · Quicker wins — preferences, hotlists, search

Preferences accumulated for years with no system of record and no policy. In one supply-constrained state: 240 providers, 49 available, and after insurance filters an IC can be down to one match or zero.

How can we convert when there is no availability?

The preferences fix went above the interface: a nationwide policy defining supportable preferences, with design surfacing them consistently. Hotlists — which ramp new clinicians whose early caseload is critical to their success — can shrink substantially by moving openings, start dates, goals, and ops logic like payer-mix caps into one place. And provider search needed to work like a directory, since outbound referrals and callers asking for someone by name come up multiple times a day per IC.

3 · Direct booking — largest single impact

Matching happened in the platform while booking lived in an EHR scheduler designed around clinic-side workflows. Recall a group number, paginate schedules, place a hold, quick-add, remove the hold, book. Minutes recoverable per call. Half the design work already existed, so re-discovery could run in parallel while insurance led.

4 · Intake capture

Chart creation, policyholder details, payment, notes, dispositions. The recommendation was sequencing: bring what finalizes a booking (chart, insurance, payment) into the platform first, end-of-call tasks later.

05 / Outcome

A roadmap the company took up.

The hypothesis became the spine of the roadmap. I packaged the inventory and priority order into a deck making the case for what the consolidated platform needed to be. Single sign-on shipped with the first-generation rollout — the analytics prerequisite for everything else. The company took up the majority of these priorities one by one, and the platform reached the full national footprint with 99% adoption.

The part I’d underline: the regions had already discovered every real problem, fifty times over, in fifty tools. My contribution was looking at all of it at once, naming the six jobs underneath, and giving the company an order of operations it could commit to. The platform that operationalized that roadmap is covered in Matching Tool.
