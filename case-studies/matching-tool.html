№ 01 · Matching Tool

From a two-state pilot to 99% national adoption.

Matching a patient with the right clinician took intake coordinators 4–7 minutes of live hold time, cycling through five or more tools. A wrong match triggered cancellations and rematches, and the fallout landed on the coordinator. A two-state pilot — three input streams, one running issue log — surfaced 18 fixes and a bigger pattern underneath them: adoption came down to trust, the gap between what the tool displayed and what a coordinator could verify. Closing that gap fix by fix got the platform to 99%.

01 / Before

Five tools and a patient on hold.

Every match meant the same loop, with a patient waiting on the line:

  • Start in the EHR
  • Open a BI dashboard to find clinical fit
  • Check a credentialing tracker for insurance
  • Scan a hotlist for priority providers
  • Return to the EHR to schedule

Every handoff added hold time. A data mismatch cost more: a clinician asking “why aren’t you scheduling me?” while the coordinator absorbed the blame for upstream data errors.

This was one symptom of a larger pattern I later documented in full: 50+ regional tools patching the same underlying gaps. That inventory and consolidation strategy is covered in Tool Consolidation.

02 / Role

Designer, appointed Product Owner.

I joined LifeStance in June 2021, recruited by a former teammate, after several years in health tech on the internal-tools side of a health-benefits platform. I came in already fluent in the unglamorous middle of healthcare: eligibility, credentialing, the operational machinery patients never see.

The matching platform existed in design but hadn’t shipped. I was appointed Product Owner in addition to my design role to change that: owning the backlog and priorities, writing tickets, running refinement, and designing the features, one role feeding the other.

03 / Pilot

Trust was the real blocker.

Two states. A group of superuser coordinators. Three input streams: shadowing, interviews, and a running issue log. The pilot surfaced 18 issues to fix before wider rollout, and a more important pattern underneath them. Coordinator hesitation came in three flavors:

  • “The tool feels incomplete.” Some care categories returned no results at all.
  • “The tool shows wrong results.” A sort that didn’t sort correctly. The single most-used filter, not working.
  • “I don’t fully trust even the correct results.” Coordinators had seen clinician data errors go unfixed before, so they double-checked everything.

One coordinator put the whole adoption problem in a sentence:

I am in a deep, loving friendship with the tool but don’t really trust her all the way yet. Sometimes I do manual searches to confirm, but I’m trusting more and more each day.

That’s the textbook arc of learning to trust an automated system: a verification phase, where the user double-checks the system until they trust it enough to stop. The design response followed directly:

  • Accuracy and completeness fixes outranked new features.
  • An accessible error-reporting path became a standing priority, because every unfixed error a coordinator finds resets the trust clock.
04 / Rollout

Skeptics retiring their spreadsheets.

State by state, the platform reached the full national footprint at 99% adoption. The survey numbers held at scale: 147 of 178 rated it “much better” than their prior tools. The feedback that mattered most came from teams that had been skeptical early:

Much easier scheduling process for us! Reduces the stress level immensely!

The BI matching report, the tool at the center of the old loop, was retired outright.

05 / Outcome

Shipped, then rolled out.

The main priority was getting the platform out into the world. It shipped, and the rollout landed at 99% adoption across 33 states. The legacy BI matching report was retired outright. Handle time dropped roughly 30 seconds per call, validated against a measured baseline. From that reduction I modeled the labor savings at ~$500K/yr in reclaimed coordinator time at national call volume, plus the booking capacity that time opens up. The dollar figure is potential, not booked, and I’d say so in the room.

What this project consolidated was narrow and deliberate: the coordinator’s check for parity and availability. It didn’t replace the 50+ regional tools — it made sure a coordinator could get accurate, current answers about who fits and who’s available in one place, so the rest of the call could move. The broader consolidation strategy — folding more workflows into the platform over time — is covered in Tool Consolidation.

06 / Reflection

What stuck with me.

Adoption came down to the gap between what the tool displayed and what coordinators could verify. Closing that gap, fix by fix, is what won the rollout — and it’s the lesson I carry into any system that asks users to trust automated decisions.
