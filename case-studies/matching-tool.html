№ 02 · Matching Tool

Getting operations staff out of Microsoft tools.

Before this project, a significant portion of provider matching at LifeStance happened inside Microsoft tools — a shared spreadsheet for overflow and exceptions, an Outlook calendar for scheduling, a handful of third-party tools around the edges, and a lot of tribal knowledge. A call-center agent might bounce between four surfaces to get a single patient booked. The ROI of moving our operations staff into a purpose-built internal product was enormous — and, at first, invisible to everyone except the agents doing the work.

01 / Context

A spreadsheet doing the work of a product.

When I started on this, the matching workflow wasn’t one tool. It was a system: a call-center booking surface, a third-party scheduling tool, a shared spreadsheet used to track overflow and exceptions, and — always — a Slack channel where the weird cases ended up.

That arrangement worked, technically. It had evolved over years of clever workarounds. But every agent was doing the invisible job of translating between surfaces, and every new hire had to learn the unwritten rules. Seconds leaked everywhere.

My job was to consolidate that workflow into a single first-class tool — and, crucially, to do it without breaking the booking operation that the organization depended on daily.

02 / Approach

Sit with the agents first.

I spent the first several weeks listening to calls and shadowing agents on video. The time we were losing wasn’t going to come from one obvious villain. It was going to come from eight or ten small places — a redundant confirmation screen, a search that required two clicks where one would do, a calendar view that required scrolling to find the right week, a spreadsheet tab that only one senior agent knew how to update correctly.

The agents were generous with their time and had clear opinions. One told me, flatly: “I don’t need you to make it pretty. I need you to stop making me wait for the provider list to load.”

03 / Moves

Where the seconds actually were.

A non-exhaustive list of the changes that added up:

  • Absorbed the overflow spreadsheet into the tool itself — every case that used to live in a cell now has a record, a state, and an owner.
  • Collapsed the provider search and the calendar into a single screen, with availability pre-loaded.
  • Removed a redundant insurance-verification step that most agents were clicking through without reading.
  • Added a keyboard-first flow for the ~30% of agents who prefer hotkeys; the fastest agents now never touch a mouse during booking.
  • Defaulted the timezone to the patient’s, not the agent’s. A small change that removed a recurring source of error and apology.
  • Replaced a blocking error state on insurance mismatch with a soft warning, allowing the agent to continue and resolve it at the end of the call.

None of these are individually novel. The lesson, maybe, is that the ROI of internal tooling lives in the aggregation of mundane fixes — not in one hero feature.

04 / Rollout

33 states, one lever at a time.

Rolling out a tool change across 33 states is not a design exercise. It’s a change-management exercise with a design artifact in the middle of it. I worked closely with training and call-center operations to sequence the rollout by region, starting with the two states with the highest call volume and the most tenured agents — the ones most likely to identify real problems versus new-tool friction.

The internal adoption playbook we built in that process — including a measurable framework for separating “new tool, same problem” from “new tool, new problem” — has since been reused for other internal tool launches.

05 / Outcome

The financial case for design.

The $500K annual savings number is the hard one — directly attributable to reduced average call time. The $15–25M in unlocked revenue capacity is softer but defensible: it represents the additional appointments the reclaimed agent time makes possible, valued at average visit revenue.

The broader effect, and the one I’m more interested in, is that this project gave the design team a financial vocabulary. It was the largest single entry on the ROI model I later built for the design function — the one covered in the Design ROI case study.
